Extending the physics-informed method

Does the physics help as much in other architectures?

The main report showed that adding a physics term to an MLP ($\text{Loss} = \text{Loss}_\text{data} + \lambda\cdot\text{Loss}_\text{physics}$) improves density prediction on wells the model never saw. After a LinkedIn comment suggested trying the idea on gradient boosting and a sequential network, and giving the model a bad-hole signal (caliper), I took both suggestions seriously and measured them rigorously.

This experiment isn't about «which one wins». The already-published MLP/PINN stays as the reference, not a rival. What I wanted to know is more specific: does that same physics term transfer to XGBoost and to an LSTM, or was it something particular to the MLP? For the comparison to be fair, everything runs on the report's same protocol.

Project invariant. In all three architectures, $\lambda=0$ reproduces the plain (data-only) model exactly. The physics term is an add-on to that model, never a different architecture. So the improvement from $\lambda=0$ to $\lambda>0$ isolates what the physics contributes.

How I set it up

Three pieces, identical across the three models, so the only real variable is the architecture and the value of $\lambda$:

  • Caliper as an input. I added a 6th feature, DCAL_NORM (the differential caliper, normalized per well). It's the «enlarged borehole» signal the comment asked for: where the hole is washed out, the measured density is unreliable and the model can learn to distrust it.
  • The same physics term, per architecture. The calibrated density–neutron relation ($\text{DEN} = A\cdot\text{NPHI} + D\cdot(\text{NPHI}\times\text{GR})$) enters each model in its own way: in the LSTM as a loss summand (just like the MLP); in XGBoost as a custom objective with gradient and Hessian ($g = (\hat y - y) + \lambda w(\hat y - f_\text{phys})$, $h = 1 + \lambda w$). The weight $w$ is the PINN's: it switches the physics off where the caliper flags washout.
  • The same evaluation protocol. Leave-One-Well-Out over 27 wells, the same 3 blind wells (seed=42), leak-free per-well normalization, metrics in g/cc. None of this changes from the report.

On complexity: the models are deliberately modest, comparable in robustness to the 5→64→64→32→1 MLP. The LSTM is 1 layer, hidden=64, windows of 32 depth samples that don't cross well boundaries. XGBoost runs with max_depth=5, eta=0.05, 400 trees. I wasn't after the biggest model, but the cleanest comparison.

On logs being noisy. A well measurement isn't an exact value: it carries tool noise and borehole-condition noise. I didn't ignore that, I treated it as data quality. The caliper weight $w$ lowers the physics' confidence exactly where the measurement is least reliable (washed-out hole), and I report the blind wells under two protocols (average of 27 models and a single model) precisely to separate how much of the gain is signal and how much is variance. Modeling the noise explicitly —a prediction with its uncertainty— would be the natural next step; it's outside the scope of this experiment.

One key decision: I didn't assume the MLP's optimal $\lambda$ (0.5) would carry over to the other architectures. I ran a per-architecture $\lambda$ sweep. It turned out to matter a lot.

Result 1 — the optimal $\lambda$ depends on the architecture

This was the most interesting part. Sweeping $\lambda$ in cross-validation (LOWO), each architecture responds to the physics differently:

Three different behaviors, and all three make sense:

  • XGBoost — the physics barely moves the needle (flat curve). It already starts from the best MAE of the three (0.1286) without physics. Gradient-boosted trees squeeze tabular structure very well, so the physics hint adds little information the model didn't already have. It doesn't hurt; it just isn't needed.
  • LSTM — monotone improvement, and the biggest winner. Without physics it's the worst (R² 0.168), but the physics rescues it to R² 0.337, and it keeps improving up to $\lambda=2$, much higher than the MLP's 0.5. A sequential network with few wells is the one that most needs an external compass, and it uses it to the fullest.
  • MLP — the familiar middle ground. A clear improvement that levels off near $\lambda=0.5$ (the operational value chosen for per-well robustness, not just the mean).
✓ There's no universal $\lambda$. The harder the problem is for the architecture on its own, the more physics weight it admits and the more it gains. Copying the MLP's $\lambda$ onto the LSTM would have left almost all the improvement on the table.

Result 2 — the real test: 3 blind wells

LOWO measures generalization within the field. The acid test is the 3 wells set aside from the start, which no model ever saw. As in the report, I predict them two ways —averaging the 27 LOWO models (ensemble) and with a single model trained on the 27 wells— as a robustness check.

✓ The most telling case is the single-model LSTM: without physics it barely generalizes (R² 0.024) and with physics it jumps to R² 0.287, on par with the rest. The physics doesn't give the model more power, it gives it a compass when a new well's data isn't enough. In XGBoost the external gain is small but consistent, in line with its flat curve.

Did the caliper input help?

The other half of the comment was giving the model the bad-hole signal. XGBoost lets us see, unambiguously, how much each input weighs in its decisions:

The caliper shows up with a modest but real weight: the model uses it to modulate its confidence where the borehole is enlarged, which is exactly the point. It isn't the dominant feature —neutron and gamma ray still lead, as the physics demands— but it adds the data-quality context that wasn't there before.

What I take away

  • The physics-informed method transfers. It wasn't an MLP trick: across all three architectures $\lambda>0$ matches or beats $\lambda=0$, hurting none of them. It's a method idea, not one specific to a single model.
  • The optimal $\lambda$ is architecture-specific and correlates with how hard the problem is for each model alone: flat in XGBoost, moderate in the MLP, high and very profitable in the LSTM. You can't copy a $\lambda$ across architectures.
  • The caliper input contributes a data-quality context the model genuinely uses, without displacing the physical signals.
  • Honesty about the scope. The external gains are modest in absolute terms —the challenge is still generalizing to a new well with few training wells— but the direction is consistent across the whole experiment.
